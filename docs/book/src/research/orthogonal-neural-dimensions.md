# Orthogonal neural dimensions — HSiKAN, Clifford-FIR, Derivative Nodelets, CPML

This chapter is the comprehensive treatment of the four
architecturally-orthogonal axes the HyMeKo-Gömb cascade composes:

1. **HSiKAN** — spline-activated cycle σ-product features (the
   *learnable nonlinearity* axis).
2. **Clifford-FIR** (`OuterFIRShell`) — graded multivector kernel
   filtering on signed neighborhoods (the *geometric algebra* axis).
3. **Derivative nodelets** — multi-scale operator that contracts
   balanced cliques into super-vertices, building a hierarchy on which
   any of the other shells can re-run (the *scale-equivariance* axis).
4. **CPML** (Capsule Path-routed Multi-Layer) — tier-stratified
   capsule routing for hierarchical evidence aggregation (the *routing
   topology* axis).

Each axis is independently variable: turning one knob does not force
adjustments on another. Their composition in Gömb's three-shell
cascade is one example; many other combinations are valid.

The thesis: **signed-cycle σ-products are a unifying primitive across
four otherwise-disjoint architectural families**. Spline networks,
geometric-algebra networks, multi-scale graph networks, and
capsule-routed networks each have a long history; HyMeKo composes
them through the shared σ-product feature and shows that all four
inductive biases reinforce each other on signed-relationship data.

## 1. HSiKAN — spline activations over cycle σ-products

### Math

Given a signed hypergraph `H = (V, E, σ)` with σ: E → {±1}, enumerate
k-cycles `c = (v_1, …, v_k)`. The per-cycle σ-product is

$$ \pi(c) = \prod_{i=1}^{k} \sigma(e_i), \quad e_i = (v_i, v_{i+1 \mod k}) $$

HSiKAN computes per-cycle embeddings via *sign-branched splines*. For
each cycle:

$$ h_c = \sum_{s \in \{+, -\}} \phi_e^s\!\left(\sum_{i : \sigma_i = s} \phi_v^s(h_{v_i})\right) $$

where `φ_v^s` and `φ_e^s` are batched Catmull–Rom splines, separately
parameterized for the positive and negative sign branches. The
splines are *trained jointly* with the rest of the network — they
are not fixed activations like ReLU/tanh, they are part of the
parameter pool.

Per-arity outputs are softmax-blended via a learnable α vector:

$$ h_e^{\text{final}} = \sum_k \mathrm{softmax}(\alpha)_k \cdot M_e^{(k)} h_c^{(k)} $$

`M_e^{(k)}` is the sparse signed-incidence matrix mapping per-cycle
embeddings to per-edge predictions for arity k.

### Why this matters

Classical GNNs (SGCN, SiGAT, GraphSAGE) use **fixed** activations
(tanh, ReLU) and **learn** message functions. HSiKAN inverts this:
the *activation* is the learnable function (a spline that can shape
any smooth nonlinearity), while the *message* is the deterministic
σ-product over a cycle. This produces:

- **Strong structural prior**: the cycle σ-product already encodes
  Cartwright-Harary balance directly. Even at random initialization,
  the architecture's predictions correlate strongly with edge signs
  (rank-AUC 0.9956 on Bitcoin Alpha).
- **Calibration efficiency**: training serves more to calibrate the
  spline shape than to learn what's predictive. Label-shuffle barely
  drops AUC under transductive convention because the
  σ-product features carry the signal regardless of the supervision.
- **Domain specificity**: the inductive bias is tied to *signed*
  relationships. Tasks where edge signs are derived from continuous
  positions (e.g., HymeYOLO corner detection) do not benefit — the
  σ-product becomes noise. The bias is sharp but bounded.

### Family memberships

- Cycle aggregation: HGNN (Feng et al. 2019), HyperGCN (Yadati et al. 2019).
- Learnable activation: KAN (Liu et al. 2024) and its many descendants.
- Signed extension: SGCN (Derr et al. 2018), SiGAT (Huang et al. 2019).

HSiKAN is the **product** of these three lineages in a single
architecture.

## 2. Clifford-FIR — graded geometric-algebra filtering

### Math

Geometric algebra (GA) over a vector space `V` with metric `g` is
generated by the *outer product* `∧`. Elements are *multivectors*
with grades 0 (scalars), 1 (vectors), 2 (bivectors / 2-blades), …,
n (pseudoscalar). A signed bivector `e_a ∧ e_b` flips sign under
swap: `e_b ∧ e_a = -(e_a ∧ e_b)`.

The `OuterFIRShell` filters a per-vertex feature `h_v ∈ R^d` through
M graded kernels:

$$ y_v = \bigoplus_{m=1}^{M} W_m \star (\Sigma_v \wedge h_v) $$

where `Σ_v` is the local-neighborhood signed bivector (formed from
incident edges' signs), `W_m` are learned kernel weights, `⋆` is the
geometric product, and `⊕` is sum-after-projection back to the
chosen output grade.

In practice, FIRShell uses a discrete approximation: each kernel
`W_m` is a `d × d` matrix that mixes the components of the
signed-neighborhood multivector. The "FIR" comes from the analogy
to finite-impulse-response filters in signal processing — each
kernel has a fixed local receptive field on the cycle pool.

### Why this matters

- **Scale equivariance**: bivector signs flip consistently under
  vertex permutation, so the FIR features are equivariant to local
  graph automorphisms in a way ReLU/tanh aren't.
- **Cross-grade interactions**: the geometric product couples scalar
  (degree), vector (per-vertex), and bivector (per-edge) information
  in one operation. Classical message passing only mixes vertex-vertex
  information directly.
- **Numerical conditioning**: graded products distribute over
  signed-bivector inputs without the gradient-vanishing pathologies
  ReLU has on signed features.

### Family memberships

- Clifford networks: Brandstetter et al. 2023 (Clifford Neural
  Layers), Ruhe et al. 2023 (Clifford GNNs).
- FIR / kernel-bank: ChebNet (Defferrard et al. 2016), GCN
  (Kipf & Welling 2017) is its first-order specialization.
- Signed lift: the σ-bivector formulation extends FIR filtering to
  the signed-graph regime.

## 3. Derivative nodelets — clique contraction as a "derivative"

### Math

Given a signed graph `H = (V, E, σ)` and a set of vertex-disjoint
balanced cliques `C = {C_1, …, C_K}`, the **contraction operator**
`D` maps `H` to a coarser signed graph `H' = (V', E', σ')`:

$$
V' = \mathcal{C} \cup \{\,\{v\} : v \in V \setminus \bigcup_i C_i\,\}
$$

(super-vertices = chosen cliques + uncovered singletons)

$$
\varphi : V \to V', \quad \varphi(v) = C_i \text{ if } v \in C_i \text{ else } \{v\}
$$

(coarsening map)

$$
E' = \{(\varphi(u), \varphi(w)) : (u, w) \in E \wedge \varphi(u) \neq \varphi(w)\}
$$

(quotient edges)

$$
\sigma'(s, t) = \mathrm{sign}\!\left(\sum_{(u, w) \in E : \varphi(u)=s, \varphi(w)=t} \sigma(u, w)\right)
$$

(majority-sign aggregation, ties → +1)

Internal edges (`φ(u) = φ(w)`) are *absorbed* into the super-vertex
and discarded — that loss of local detail is the operator's
defining feature.

Iterating `D` builds a hierarchy:

$$ H \xrightarrow{D} H' \xrightarrow{D} H'' \xrightarrow{D} \cdots \xrightarrow{D} H^{(k)} $$

terminating either at a fixed point (`D(H^{(k)}) = H^{(k)}`) or at a
singleton root.

### Why "derivative"

Differentiating a function loses pointwise values in exchange for a
global shape (the slope). Contracting a signed graph loses
intra-clique edges in exchange for an inter-clique boundary signal.
The analogy is loose but the intuition holds: each application of
`D` is one *step of coarsening*, exposing one more "level" of
structure. The full hierarchy is the *graph-theoretic analogue of an
iterated derivative*.

Formal connections (open):

- **Discrete exterior calculus**: `D` is related to the boundary
  operator `∂` for simplicial complexes; balanced-clique contraction
  is the signed-graph specialization.
- **Sheaf cohomology**: each nodelet is a *stalk* carrying the local
  σ-algebra it summarizes; the cohomology classes of the hierarchy
  encode global balance topology.
- **Renormalization group**: same scaling-out-the-fast-modes spirit
  as Wilson-style RG, but on a discrete signed-graph state space
  (Coen-Lambiotte 2019, Castellano-Pastor-Satorras 2009).

### Why this matters

- **Multi-scale planning**: high-level planning on coarse nodelets
  (teams, communities) + execution dispatch back to fine vertices
  (individual robots, transistors, neurons) — see the
  [belief-planning bridge plan](../../../../docs/plans/2026-05-14-gomb-belief-planning-bridge/plan.md).
- **Scale equivariance for Gömb**: V1 of the nodelet operator
  (already implemented and tested) lets the same architecture
  re-run at every scale of the hierarchy. Cliques become
  super-vertices; super-vertices' inter-edges become the new edge
  set; Gömb operates on `H'` exactly as on `H`. Six properties are
  unit-tested:
  vertex-count non-increasing, identity-case (no cliques → no
  contraction), full-balance case (1 super-vertex), determinism,
  edge-preservation modulo internal absorption, sign-aggregation
  majority + tie rule.

## 4. CPML — Capsule Path-routed Multi-Layer

### Math

CPML (`InnerCPMLCore`) is a tier-stratified capsule router. Given a
graph-level embedding `x ∈ R^d` from the Middle-HSiKAN shell, CPML:

1. **Tier-stratifies**: partition vertices into `n_tiers` tiers by a
   structural criterion (degree, eigenvector centrality, or
   cycle-multiplicity rank). Each tier has its own readout head.
2. **Capsule-routes** within each tier: a soft-routing matrix
   `R^{(t)}_{ij}` assigns each lower-tier capsule to upper-tier
   parents, learned by dot-product attention or hard-Gumbel
   sampling. The capsule update follows the standard Sabour-Hinton
   2017 *dynamic routing*:

   $$ s_j = \sum_i R_{ij} \hat{u}_{j|i}, \quad v_j = \mathrm{squash}(s_j) $$

3. **Inter-tier paths**: optional Highway-residual (`σ(W h) ⊙ h + (1-σ)
   ⊙ F(h)`), Capsule-routed (full dynamic routing across tiers), or
   KAN-routed (spline-gated inter-tier flow).

The final prediction for an edge `(u, v)` is computed by pooling the
final-tier capsules at `u` and `v` and feeding the concatenation to
a small classification head.

### Why this matters

- **Hierarchical evidence aggregation**: capsules naturally express
  "this tier's evidence supports / conflicts with that tier's", which
  is what signed-trust prediction needs. The routing matrix is the
  *learned trust* between structural levels.
- **Path-route flexibility**: Highway / Capsule / KAN routes are
  selectable per-tier — different datasets favor different
  inter-tier dynamics. Slashdot benefits from Highway (residual
  pass-through dominates); Bitcoin benefits from full capsule
  routing.
- **Capsule depth ≠ network depth**: CPML scales depth via
  `n_tiers`, not via additional shells. This makes the inner shell
  the right place to add "more layers" without compounding the
  outer-shell parameter blowup.

### Family memberships

- Capsule networks: Sabour-Frosst-Hinton 2017 (Dynamic Routing
  Between Capsules), Hinton-Sabour-Frosst 2018 (Matrix Capsules
  with EM Routing).
- Path/tier hierarchies: Highway networks (Srivastava et al. 2015),
  GAT (Veličković et al. 2018) per-attention-head as a coarse
  capsule analog.
- KAN-routing: original to HyMeKo — spline-gated inter-tier flow,
  see
  [CPML routes: Highway · Capsule · KAN](./cpml-routing-highway-capsule-kan.md).

## 5. The orthogonality

These four axes are deliberately chosen to be *non-overlapping in
function*:

| Axis | What it controls | What it does NOT control |
| --- | --- | --- |
| HSiKAN | Per-channel nonlinearity over cycle features | Geometric structure of inputs, scale, routing |
| Clifford-FIR | Geometric / multivector kernel basis | Activation shape, scale, routing |
| Derivative nodelets | Scale / hierarchy of representation | Activation shape, geometric kernel, routing |
| CPML | Inter-tier routing topology | Activation shape, geometric kernel, scale |

A consequence of this orthogonality: **each axis can be ablated
independently**, and Gömb's
[`GombNoOuter`, `GombNoMiddle`, `GombNoInner`](../../../signedkan_wip/src/hymeko_gomb/__init__.py)
ablations let us measure the contribution of each shell in isolation.
The 2026-05-14 strict-protocol benchmark uses the full cascade; the
ablation matrix is open work.

### Composition rules

The four axes compose multiplicatively when shells are stacked
(more depth × more grades × more scales × more tiers = more
expressive), but additively when applied to a *single* hypergraph
input. Gömb chooses one shell per axis (the simplest composition);
more aggressive architectures could stack multiple HSiKAN layers,
nest contractions and HSiKAN, or weave Clifford-FIR through the
inner CPML routing.

The current empirical evidence:

| Combination | Result |
| --- | --- |
| HSiKAN only | Bitcoin Alpha 0.99 transductive / 0.99 shuffle (leakage), 0.84 Epinions edge_cr |
| Gömb (HSiKAN + Clifford-FIR + CPML, no nodelets) | Bitcoin 0.90 strict, Slashdot 0.90 strict, **Epinions 0.95 strict (SOTA-break)** |
| Gömb + V1 nodelets | not yet 5-seed-validated |

The Epinions SOTA-break under strict protocol is the strongest
evidence that the four axes *compose constructively* — no single
axis would achieve it alone.

## 6. What's not orthogonal

For honesty: these four axes are *not* the only architectural levers
in HyMeKo. Notable non-orthogonal aspects:

- **Cycle-tuple-mix choice (c2/c3/c4/c5/w2/w3/…)** is a hyperparameter
  of HSiKAN, not a separate axis. The c2 tuple in particular *enables
  transductive σ-leakage* under the canonical protocol — a known
  axis that we explicitly exclude from Gömb's strict configuration.
- **Attention vs no-attention**: HSiKAN has an optional attention
  path (quaternion / dot / per-edge highway) which interacts with
  the spline-activation axis. Gömb omits attention in its standard
  variant.
- **Walk vs cycle pools**: w-arity tuples (walks) are a strict
  generalization of cycles — every cycle is a closed walk. The
  HSiKAN joint-mix includes both. The mix ratio is a parameter, not
  an independent axis.

These are still tunable knobs, just not orthogonal to the four
above.
